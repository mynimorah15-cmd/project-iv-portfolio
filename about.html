About The Artist

Namyni is a visual artist that focuses on painting and drawing. Her art pieces are a visual study of the origins of black musical cultures and the culture she was accustomed to as a black girl raised in New Orleans. Namyni takes a lot of inspiration originally from the beginnings of Hip-Hop culture in Harlem, New York. It was not until college that she began oil painting and through her time in Baton Rouge, Namyni learned to appreciate the culture she was raised in proximity to. She has been working to improve and better express her appreciation through this new medium in a way that is not too objective or standard. She has been approaching narratives through a more abstract compositional lens, with a more intentional use of mark making and color. Namyni's time at LSU has allowed her to learn this not only from her professors, but also her fellow student artists.

The culture of music and dance that Namyni was accustomed to growing up heavily motivates her to create. From the age of three to seventeen she was technically trained in ballet, jazz, hip-hop, tap, and contemporary dance at Art In Motion Dance Academy. Her time at the dance school introduced her to a large variety of sound, as her main dance teacher has a broad music taste. The time she spent in this studio taught her how rhythm interacts with the body and how far the range of movement spans artistically. Dance is a form of expression and communication. When Namyni was growing up, she had a hard time connecting to her peers. Namyni was often referred to as one of the “weird black girls” by the other black girls in her company. Dancing was always something that felt bigger than this boundary and it opened a lot of doors for her. She was given the opportunity to perform in front of her family, at jazz fest, and competitively. Namyni has traveled around the country to compete and learn from multiple different choreographers surrounded by other dancers around the world. Through these experiences, she was invigorated and intrigued by how much dance and music as art forms could connect a coalition.

Her paintings and general artwork pulls directly from her own personal experiences with music, dance, and history. Growing up in New Orleans, Namyni experienced a lot of live band music as well as lively DJs. Every single event featured consistent, up-beat tunes and beats in the background, so the atmosphere was always filled with movement. Any family gathering that she was at always involved dancing; even in grief, they held second lines to dance in celebration of the life that was lived before it was lost. In her 20 years alive, she had never missed a Mardi Gras and was accustomed to seeing and hearing school bands practice in the street months prior. As Namyni moved away from New Orleans, she began to appreciate this origin more as a black-American subculture that she was privileged to experience. This feeling of community and movement is something that Namyni strives to present in her pieces, even if the imagery is not necessarily those specific scenes. She is intrigued with the accumulation of this culture and the many other regionally black subcultures. Not for money, fame, status, or attention; but for the love of the craft and the trust in an idea.

Namyni's goal is to encapsulate her experience and perception of life in my visual art through color and use of material alone.
