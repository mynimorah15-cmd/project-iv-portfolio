About The Artist

I am a visual artist that focuses on painting and drawing. My art pieces are a visual study of the origins of black musical cultures and the culture I was accustomed to as a black girl raised in New Orleans. I take a lot of inspiration originally from the beginnings of Hip-Hop culture in Harlem, New York. When I was in high school, I only expressed this love through graphite drawings. It was not until college that I began oil painting and through my time in Baton Rouge, I learned to appreciate the culture I was raised in proximity to. Ive been working to improve and better express my appreciation through this new medium in a way that is not too objective or standard. I have been approaching narratives through a more abstract compositional lens, with a more intentional use of mark making and color. My time at LSU has allowed me to learn this not only from my professors, but also my fellow student artists.

The culture of music and dance that I was accustomed to growing up heavily motivates me to create. From the age of three to seventeen I was technically trained in ballet, jazz, hip-hop, tap, and contemporary dance at Art In Motion Dance Academy. My time at the dance school introduced me to a large variety of sound, as our main dance teacher has a broad music taste. The time I spent in this studio taught me how rhythm interacts with movement and how far the range of movement spans artistically. Dance is a form of expression and communication. When I was growing up, I had a hard time connecting to my peers. I was always seen as one of the “weird black girls” amongst the others. Dancing was always something that felt bigger than this boundary and it opened a lot of doors for me. I was given the opportunity to perform in front of my family, at jazz fest, and competitively. I've traveled around the country to compete and learn from multiple different choreographers surrounded by other dancers around the world. Through these experiences, I was invigorated and intrigued by how much dance and music as art forms could connect a coalition.

My paintings and general artwork pulls directly from my own personal experiences with music, dance, and history. Growing up in New Orleans, I experienced a lot of live band music as well as lively DJs. Every single event had lively, up-beat tunes and beats in the background, so the atmosphere was always filled with movement. Any family gathering that I was at always involved dancing; even in grief, we hold second lines to dance in celebration of the life that was lived before it was lost. In my 20 years alive, I had never missed a Mardi Gras and was accustomed to seeing and hearing school bands practice in the street months prior. As I moved away from New Orleans, I began to appreciate this origin more as a black-American subculture that I was extremely privileged to experience. Baton Rouge is only an hour away from NOLA, but the energy doesnt come close. This feeling of community and movement is something that I strive to present in my pieces, even if the imagery is not necessarily those specific scenes. I am intrigued with the accumulation of this culture and the many other regionally black subcultures. Not for money, fame, status, or attention; but for the love of the craft and the trust in an idea.

My goal is to encapsulate my experience and perception of life in my visual art through color and use of material.
